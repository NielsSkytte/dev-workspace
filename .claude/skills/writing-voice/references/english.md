# English - voice specifics

> The reader is still a Danish/Nordic business customer, usually a non-native English speaker (Melbye is Swedish). Write plain, clear, international English. Treat Danish as the reference voice and **port its warmth and structure into English** - his English is fluent but a register flatter, so reinforce the warmth rather than sanding it off. The universal rules and the strict offer layer still apply on top.

## Plain English for a Nordic reader

- Avoid idiom, heavy phrasal verbs, and culture-bound references. Prefer the direct verb: "use", not "leverage"; "set up", not "stand up" (figurative); "reduce", not "dial down" in formal copy.
- Short, self-contained sentences scan better for a second-language reader and suit his natural English rhythm. Split run-ons.
- Keep EU framing precise: data residency, GDPR, "outside the EU region" - these are substance to this reader.

## Greeting and sign-off

- **Greeting:** recipient-driven. "Hi <name>" for an established relationship; "Dear <name>" for a formal or first-contact message. (Unlike Danish, he does climb to "Dear" in English.)
- **Sign-off:** "Kind regards, Niels Skytte" is the English default.

## The one-way valve - scrub Danish leaks

English-into-Danish code-switching is on-brand (English terms inside Danish prose). Danish-into-English is **not**. The highest-value fix: he sometimes signs an otherwise-English email "Mvh" out of muscle memory. Scrub any Danish that slips into English output:

- "Mvh" -> "Kind regards"
- "inkl." -> "incl." / "including"
- Danish connectives or "f.eks." -> "for example", "e.g."

## Preserve, do not over-native-ize

His mildly formal, slightly literal phrasings read as courteous and professional, and they are part of his authentic, trustworthy English. **Keep them** - sanding them into idiomatic native English makes the text sound generic-AI, not like Niels:

- "would suit us fine"
- "When would you have time for us to come by"
- "the coming week"
- "good to hear that it was not an issue on our side"

## Port in the invariants

- Appreciation-first opener: "Thank you for your email." / "Thanks for sending the brief."
- The one-sentence ask, stated plainly.
- First-person ownership of recommendations: "my recommendation is", "we'd suggest".
- Pressure-defusing reassurance where the situation is tense.
- Candor about risk and limits over false certainty.

## English connectives

- **Prefer:** so, since, which, but, however, then.
- **Avoid:** moreover, furthermore, in addition, additionally - the additive ladder he never uses.
- Cohesion by reaching back ("This means...", "That leaves...") rather than by piling on connectors.
